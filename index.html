“Our framework also connects to the literature on financial networks, which studies how the structure of inter-firm financial linkages shapes the propagation of shocks (e.g., Acemoglu, Ozdaglar, and Tahbaz-Salehi, 2015; Elliott, Golub, and Jackson, 2014; Cabrales, Gottardi, and Vega-Redondo, 2017). This generates propagation driven by expectations about future relationship values rather than by the network topology of existing obligations.”

+ Claude suggestion

We address R4’s three criteria, focusing on (a) and (b).

On (a): Our micro-foundation resolves a genuine conflict in the existing quantitative literature. Shao (2025) finds that trade credit amplifies financial shocks, while Reischer (2024) finds that it smooths them. This contradiction reflects different assumptions about what governs the trade credit limit, not different calibrations. Our micro-foundation derives the constraint from limited commitment: it binds on the present value of the relationship, generating a state-dependent response—trade credit smooths when suppliers are unconstrained and amplifies when they are constrained. The direction is an endogenous prediction, not an assumption. Our calibration places Italy in the amplification region.

On (b): Because the constraint is forward-looking, current trade credit responds to changes in expectations about future conditions. Section 3.3.4 shows that a tightening of buyer borrowing capacity reduces trade credit even when suppliers are unaffected—because the relationship value J falls. This channel is absent from static frameworks. The micro-foundation also generates a novel policy implication (Section 5.4): subsidies to suppliers can be more effective than subsidies to customers, because customer subsidies weaken repayment incentives. This result cannot arise with reduced-form constraints.
